The 674-Mile Relay That Actually Saved Nome

In January 1925, diphtheria broke out in Nome, Alaska. The town’s supply of antitoxin was running out, and the nearest supply was in Nenana, 674 miles away. The rail line ended in Nenana. From there, the serum had to be carried by dog sled teams through a blizzard that was actively raging. The temperature dropped to -50 degrees Fahrenheit. Visibility was zero. The lead musher, Gunnar Kaasen, was following Balto into Nome, but he was not the one who had carried the serum across the most dangerous section of the route.

The relay involved 20 teams. Each team carried the serum for roughly 30 to 50 miles, passing it to the next team at designated checkpoints. The serum was kept in a wooden box, insulated with wool blankets, and carried on the back of the lead sled. The dogs ran in a fan formation, with the lead dog setting the pace and the rest of the team following in his tracks, breaking the wind and the snow. This is not a romanticized image. This is the exact working formation used by Arctic indigenous peoples for centuries, and it is the only way to move heavy loads over deep snow without exhausting the animals.

Togo’s leg was 114 miles long, the longest of any team in the relay. But it was not the length that made it dangerous. It was the terrain. Togo had to cross Norton Sound, a 10-mile stretch of open water between the Seward Peninsula and the mainland. The ice was thin, cracking under the weight of the sled, and the wind was pushing the dogs toward the open water. If the ice gave way, the serum would be lost, and the dogs would drown. Togo did not just cross the sound. He led his team across it three times, because the ice was too thin to support the full weight of the sled and the serum on a single crossing. He broke the ice, swam through the open water, and led his team back to the solid ice, repeating the process until the serum was safely across. No other dog in the relay faced this specific, lethal combination of conditions.

Why Togo Was Not Just a Mutt

Togo was not a purebred. He was not a Siberian Husky, and he was not a Malamute. He was a mixed-breed dog, the offspring of a Siberian Husky and a wolf-dog hybrid. Most people today look at a mixed-breed dog and see a pet. In 1925, Leonhard Seppala looked at Togo and saw a working dog. Seppala was a Norwegian musher who had moved to Alaska to work with sled dogs, and he had a very specific idea of what a working dog should be. He did not care about pedigree. He cared about performance. He cared about the dog’s ability to read the terrain, to make decisions on his own, and to push through conditions that would break a less capable animal.

Togo had a high prey drive, which is the instinct to chase and hunt small animals. This is not a flaw in a working dog. This is the engine that drives a dog to run for hours without stopping. ]]> In a shelter intake room in Portland, a five-year-old mixed-breed dog named Barnaby sits perfectly still while a volunteer offers him a treat. He does not take it. He does not move away from it. He simply stares at the floor, his ears pinned back, his tail tucked tight against his belly. The volunteer marks him as ‘easygoing’ and ‘low-maintenance’ in the adoption file. Three months later, Barnaby is still there. And every day, he stares at the floor.

Shelters call this ‘good behavior.’ It is not. It is suppression, and it is the single most common reason dogs remain in shelters longer than they should. A dog who does not bark, does not jump, does not pull on the leash, and does not beg for attention is not a well-adjusted dog. He is a dog who has learned that any action he takes will be met with discomfort, rejection, or pain. He has learned to shut down. And because he is quiet, he is overlooked.

This is the adoption myth that keeps dogs in shelters: the belief that a quiet dog is a good dog. The reality is that a quiet dog is often a suppressed dog. And until shelters and adopters learn to read the difference, dogs like Barnaby will keep waiting for a home that does not exist.

What Suppression Actually Looks Like

Suppression is not the same as training. Training is the process of teaching a dog what to do. Suppression is the process of teaching a dog what not to do, and it leaves the dog with no alternatives. When a dog is repeatedly punished, ignored, or startled for normal canine behaviors, he learns to stop performing them. He does not learn a replacement behavior. He simply stops.

Suppressed dogs display a specific set of body language cues that experienced handlers recognize immediately. They are often mistaken for calmness by first-time adopters.

  • Avoidance of eye contact. A trained dog will make soft, intermittent eye contact with their handler. A suppressed dog will actively avoid looking at people, often staring at the floor or turning his head away.
  • Freezing. When approached, a suppressed dog will stand perfectly still, even if he is uncomfortable. He will not move toward or away from the stimulus. He will simply wait for it to pass.
  • Submissive urination. This is not a housebreaking issue. It is an involuntary stress response triggered by direct eye contact, reaching over the dog’s head, or looming over him.
  • Refusal to take food. A healthy, confident dog will take food from a hand. A suppressed dog will often refuse food when a stranger is present, or will take it extremely slowly, scanning the room the entire time.
  • Yawning, lip-licking, and whale eye. These are displacement behaviors. They signal anxiety, not boredom. A dog who yawns repeatedly when no one is tired, or licks his lips when no food is present, is communicating stress.

When a shelter staff member sees a dog who does none of these things, they assume the dog is fine. They do not see the dog who is actively trying not to exist in the space. They do not see the dog who has learned that the safest thing he can do is nothing at all.

Why Shelters Reward Suppression

Shelters are operating under extreme resource constraints. Every kennel has a limit. Every staff member is stretched thin. Every dog costs money to house, feed, and care for. In this environment, a dog who does not bark, does not pace, and does not pull on the leash is a logistical miracle. He is easy to manage. He is easy to market. He is easy to adopt out.

Shelter staff are not trained to recognize suppression. They are trained to manage risk. A barking dog is a risk to neighbors and staff. A pacing dog is a risk to his own health. A pulling dog is a risk to the handler. A quiet dog is no risk. Therefore, a quiet dog is a good dog.

This is a false equivalence. A dog who is quiet because he is trained is different from a dog who is quiet because he is terrified. The former has options. The latter does not. And yet, shelters have no incentive to distinguish between the two. The faster a dog gets adopted, the faster the kennel opens for the next dog. Speed matters more than depth. And a suppressed dog is the fastest dog to adopt.

The result is a system that actively filters out the dogs who need the most help. The loud dogs, the anxious dogs, the dogs who pull and jump and bark, are the ones who get adopted first. Not because they are better dogs. Because they are louder. They are demanding attention. They are forcing the system to see them. And in doing so, they escape the kennel.

The quiet dogs are left behind. Not because they are bad dogs. Because they are good at being invisible.

How to Spot a Suppressed Dog Before You Adopt

If you are visiting a shelter, you need to look past the behavior and read the body language. Here is what to do.

Do not approach the dog directly. Sit on the floor. Turn your body slightly sideways. Let the dog come to you. Watch what he does. A confident dog will approach you, sniff your hand, and make eye contact. A suppressed dog will approach you slowly, avoid eye contact, and may not take your hand at all. He may simply sit beside you and stare at the floor.

Test his response to touch. Reach toward the dog slowly, from the side, not from above. A confident dog will relax into the touch. A suppressed dog will flinch, freeze, or pull away. He may not growl. He may not snap. He will simply shut down. This is not aggression. This is fear.

Offer a treat and watch him take it. Place a treat on the floor near the dog, not in your hand. A confident dog will take it immediately. A suppressed dog will hesitate. He may sniff it, look up at you, look back at the floor, and then slowly take it. Or he may not take it at all. This is not pickiness. This is anxiety.

Leave the room and watch what he does. This is the most revealing test. Walk out of the kennel and close the door. Watch the dog through the wire. A confident dog will settle down, lie down, and relax. A suppressed dog will often become more anxious. He may pace. He may bark. He may press himself against the back wall. He may stare at the door. This is separation distress. It is not a sign of a bad dog. It is a sign of a dog who has learned that people leave.

If you see these signs, do not walk away. This dog needs you. He needs someone who understands that his silence is not compliance. It is survival.

What to Do After You Bring Him Home

Bringing a suppressed dog home is not about training. It is about rebuilding trust. And trust is rebuilt slowly, through consistency, patience, and the willingness to let the dog set the pace.

Do not force interaction. Let the dog come to you. Do not pick him up. Do not hug him. Do not stare at him. Sit on the floor and read a book. Let him observe you. Let him decide when he is ready to approach you. This may take hours. It may take days. It may take weeks. Do not rush it.

Use food to rebuild positive associations. Place high-value treats on the floor near you. Do not throw them. Do not call him. Simply place them and walk away. Let him come to you when he is ready. This teaches him that your presence predicts good things, not discomfort.

Respect his boundaries. If he moves away, let him. If he does not want to be touched, do not touch him. If he is hiding, do not pull him out. Give him space. Give him options. Give him the chance to choose you, rather than forcing you onto him.

Be patient with housebreaking. Suppressed dogs often struggle with housebreaking. They are so focused on not making mistakes that they forget to signal when they need to go. Do not punish accidents. Do not rub their nose in it. Do not yell. Simply clean it up and try again. This is not stubbornness. This is fear.

Consider professional help. If your dog is severely suppressed, consider working with a certified behavior consultant who specializes in fear and anxiety. They can help you create a structured plan to rebuild his confidence and trust. This is not a sign of failure. It is a sign of commitment.

Why This Matters Beyond the Shelter

Suppression is not a shelter problem. It is a dog problem. Every dog who has been punished, ignored, or startled for normal canine behaviors has the capacity to become suppressed. Every dog who has learned that the safest thing he can do is nothing at all carries that lesson into his next home.

When we adopt a suppressed dog, we are not adopting a quiet dog. We are adopting a dog who has learned to survive. And survival is not living. It is waiting. It is hoping. It is praying that the next person will be different.

If you are looking for a dog who will not bark, will not pull, will not jump, will not beg, will not demand attention, look harder. Look past the silence. Look past the stillness. Look for the dog who is trying not to exist. He is the one who needs you most. And he is the one who will love you the deepest, once he learns that he is allowed to exist.

Do not adopt a dog because he is quiet. Adopt a dog because you understand what his silence means. And then, do the work to help him find his voice.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my dog is suppressed?

Watch his body language, not his behavior. If he avoids eye contact, freezes when approached, urinates involuntarily when touched, refuses food from strangers, or displays displacement behaviors like yawning and lip-licking, he is likely suppressed. These are stress signals, not calmness.

Can a suppressed dog ever become confident?

Yes. With patience, consistency, and the right environment, suppressed dogs can rebuild their confidence. The key is to never force interaction, always let the dog set the pace, and use food to create positive associations. This process can take weeks, months, or even years, depending on the severity of the suppression.

Should I adopt a suppressed dog?

If you are an experienced dog owner who understands body language, has the time and patience to work with a fearful dog, and is committed to never forcing interaction, then yes. If you are looking for an easy dog who will not demand attention, then no. A suppressed dog requires work, not a quiet kennel.

What should I avoid doing with a suppressed dog?

Avoid forcing eye contact, reaching over his head, picking him up, hugging him, staring at him, punishing accidents, or pulling him out of hiding. These actions reinforce his belief that human interaction equals discomfort. Instead, sit on the floor, let him come to you, and use food to build positive associations.

How long does it take for a suppressed dog to recover?

There is no fixed timeline. Some dogs begin to show signs of confidence within weeks. Others take months. Some never fully recover, but they can still live happy, fulfilling lives with the right owner. The key is to measure progress by the dog’s willingness to engage, not by a calendar.

]]> Double-coated dogs shed year-round. Not in seasonal waves, not in predictable clumps, but in a steady, quiet drip that makes owners believe the coat is broken, the dog is unwell, or they are doing something wrong. The truth is simpler and less dramatic: the undercoat does not shed in waves. It sheds continuously, and the guard hairs trap that undercoat until the dog moves, rubs, or gets brushed. The result is not a shedding problem. It is a climate-regulation system operating exactly as it evolved to.

That distinction matters because most owners treat the symptom, not the mechanism. They buy deshedding shampoos, they brush aggressively, they shave the dog in summer, and they wonder why the coat grows back patchy or why the dog seems colder in winter. The double coat is not a defect to be managed. It is a functional system. Understanding how it actually works changes every grooming decision from reactive to proactive.

The Two-Layer Architecture: What Each Coat Actually Does

A double coat consists of two distinct layers that serve different purposes. The outer layer, called guard hairs or the topcoat, is longer, coarser, and water-resistant. These hairs form the visible surface of the coat and provide the primary barrier against weather, debris, and UV exposure. The inner layer, the undercoat, is shorter, denser, and insulating. It sits close to the skin and traps air to regulate body temperature.

The guard hairs do not shed in cycles. They grow to a genetically predetermined length, then fall out individually as part of normal wear and tear. The undercoat, however, operates on a different timeline. It grows in density during cooler months, then thins gradually as temperatures rise. This thinning process is continuous, not seasonal. The dog is shedding undercoat hairs every single day, but because the guard hairs cover them, the owner rarely sees it until the dog rubs against furniture, rolls on the floor, or gets brushed.

This continuous shedding pattern is why double-coated breeds like Golden Retrievers, Siberian Huskies, German Shepherds, and Norwegian Elkhounds appear to shed constantly. They are not shedding more than other dogs. They are shedding in a way that is hidden by the topcoat. The guard hairs act as a net, catching the loose undercoat until physical movement dislodges it. That is why you see clumps of fur on your couch after the dog has been lying there, not because the dog suddenly decided to shed, but because the movement finally released what had been trapped.

Why Shaving a Double Coat Backfires

The most common mistake owners make with double-coated dogs is shaving them. The logic seems sound: less coat means less shedding, less heat retention, and easier maintenance. The reality is the opposite. Shaving a double coat does not reduce shedding. It disrupts the coat’s ability to regulate temperature, damages the hair follicles, and often causes the coat to grow back abnormally.

When you shave a double-coated dog, you remove both the guard hairs and the undercoat. The guard hairs take months to years to grow back, and they often grow back coarser, darker, or in patches. The undercoat, however, grows back faster and often denser than before, creating a coat that is thicker, heavier, and sheds even more than it did originally. This phenomenon is so common that it has a name: post-clipping alopecia, where the coat fails to regrow properly after shaving, and coat dysplasia, where the new growth is structurally different from the original.

More importantly, the double coat is designed to insulate against both cold and heat. The guard hairs reflect sunlight and block UV radiation, while the undercoat traps air to keep the dog cool in summer and warm in winter. Remove the guard hairs, and the dog loses its primary defense against sunburn and heatstroke. The undercoat, now exposed, absorbs more heat rather than reflecting it. This is why double-coated dogs that are shaved often seem hotter in summer, not cooler. They have lost the very mechanism that protects them from overheating.

The American Kennel Club breed standards for double-coated breeds explicitly state that shaving is discouraged. The FCI (Fédération Cynologique Internationale) breed standards reinforce this. The working purpose of these breeds, herding, sled pulling, hunting in Arctic conditions, required a coat that could handle extreme temperature swings. Shaving that coat removes the functional adaptations that breeders spent centuries refining.

The Reset Protocol: How to Manage Continuous Shedding

If the double coat sheds continuously, and shaving makes it worse, what should owners do? The answer lies in understanding the reset protocol: a structured brushing routine that removes loose undercoat before it gets trapped, without damaging the topcoat or disrupting the natural shedding cycle.

The reset protocol works on a simple principle: remove the loose undercoat regularly so it does not build up and mat against the skin. This prevents the guard hairs from trapping so much loose hair, reduces the amount of fur that ends up on your furniture, and keeps the coat functioning as a climate regulator rather than a heat trap.

Here is how to execute the reset protocol:

  • Use the right tools. A slicker brush works well for breaking up mats and removing loose guard hairs. An undercoat rake or deshedding tool (like the Furminator) reaches the undercoat without damaging the topcoat. Avoid human combs or fine-toothed brushes, which pull guard hairs and cause discomfort.
  • Brush in the direction of hair growth. Start with the guard hairs to loosen any trapped undercoat, then use the undercoat rake to reach the base. Work in small sections, especially around the neck, shoulders, and tail, where undercoat density is highest.
  • Brush twice a week during peak shedding. For most double-coated breeds, this means late winter through early summer. During non-shedding months, once a week is sufficient. Do not brush daily unless the dog has severe matting, as over-brushing can stimulate oil production and irritate the skin.
  • Follow with a rinse. After brushing, rinse the dog with cool water to wash away the loose undercoat. This prevents the fur from re-depositing on the dog’s skin or your furniture. A final coat conditioner can help smooth the guard hairs and reduce static, which makes future brushing easier.
  • Check the skin. While brushing, inspect the skin for redness, hot spots, parasites, or lumps. The undercoat hides these issues, so regular brushing is also a health check. If you find anything unusual, consult a veterinarian.

This protocol does not stop shedding. It manages it. The dog will still shed undercoat hairs continuously. But by removing the loose hair before it gets trapped, you reduce the clumps that end up on your furniture, prevent matting against the skin, and keep the coat functioning as intended. The goal is not zero shedding. The goal is a coat that works.

When the Coat Is Not Working: Health Signals to Watch For

A healthy double coat sheds continuously but remains balanced. The guard hairs grow to their genetic length, the undercoat thins and thickens gradually, and the dog’s skin is clean and free of irritation. When the coat stops functioning properly, it is often a sign of an underlying health issue, not a grooming failure.

Watch for these signals:

  • Excessive shedding. If the dog is shedding more than usual, especially if the undercoat is coming out in large clumps or patches, this could indicate stress, nutritional deficiency, thyroid imbalance, or Cushing’s disease. A veterinary checkup can rule out medical causes.
  • Dull, dry, or brittle coat. A healthy double coat has a natural sheen. If the coat looks dull, feels dry, or breaks easily, this could signal a fatty acid deficiency, poor-quality diet, or skin infection. Omega-3 and Omega-6 fatty acids are critical for coat health, and a veterinary nutritionist can recommend appropriate supplements.
  • Hot spots or bald patches. These are areas of inflamed, infected skin that occur when the undercoat mats against the skin, trapping moisture and bacteria. Hot spots require immediate veterinary attention and often topical antibiotics or antifungals. Preventing them starts with regular brushing and keeping the undercoat loose.
  • Changes in coat texture. If the guard hairs grow back coarser, darker, or in patches after shaving, or if the undercoat becomes denser and heavier than before, this is coat dysplasia. It is a direct result of disrupting the natural shedding cycle, and there is no cure other than time. The coat may never return to its original state.

These signals are not grooming failures. They are health signals. A double-coated dog’s coat is a window into its overall health. When the coat stops functioning properly, the dog is telling you something is wrong. The question is whether you listen.

The Honest Limits: When the Reset Protocol Fails

The reset protocol works for most double-coated dogs in most climates. It does not work for every dog, and it does not work in every situation. Understanding the limits of this approach is as important as understanding the protocol itself.

First, the reset protocol assumes a healthy dog with a normal shedding cycle. Dogs with medical conditions, thyroid disease, Cushing’s, allergies, skin infections, will not respond to brushing alone. The underlying health issue must be addressed first. Brushing a dog with active hot spots or severe allergies can worsen the irritation and delay healing.

Second, the reset protocol does not stop shedding. It manages it. If your goal is zero shedding, the reset protocol will fail. Double-coated dogs shed. That is their biology. The protocol reduces the visible impact, but the dog will still shed undercoat hairs continuously. Accepting this reality is part of owning a double-coated breed.

Third, the reset protocol requires time and consistency. Brushing twice a week is not a one-time event. It is a habit. Owners who skip brushing for weeks and then try to catch up with an aggressive session often cause discomfort, stress, and resistance. The dog learns to associate brushing with pain, and the process becomes harder, not easier. Consistency is the only way to make brushing manageable.

Finally, the reset protocol does not work for all breeds. Some double-coated breeds, like the Samoyed or the Keeshond, have extremely dense undercoats that require more frequent and intensive brushing than others. Some mixed-breed dogs inherit double coats without inheriting the full grooming needs, leading to unpredictable shedding patterns. There is no one-size-fits-all solution. The protocol must be adapted to the individual dog.

Why This Matters Beyond the Grooming Table

Understanding the double coat as a climate regulator, not a shedding problem, changes how you see your dog. It changes how you brush, how you dress, how you exercise, and how you interpret the fur on your furniture. It shifts the focus from control to cooperation. You are not fighting the coat. You are working with it.

This shift matters because it reduces stress for both you and your dog. When you understand that shedding is continuous, not a malfunction, you stop panicking when you see fur on your clothes. When you understand that shaving damages the coat, you stop reaching for the clippers. When you understand that the reset protocol is about management, not elimination, you stop chasing perfection and start building sustainable habits.

The double coat is not a flaw. It is a masterpiece of evolutionary engineering. It keeps the dog warm in winter, cool in summer, protected from the elements, and insulated against temperature swings. It requires maintenance, yes, but that maintenance is not a burden. It is a privilege. You get to work with a system that has been refined over thousands of years of selective breeding. The least you can do is respect it.

So the next time you see a clump of undercoat on your couch, do not panic. Do not reach for the shampoo, the razor, or the guilt. Your dog is not broken. The coat is not broken. The system is working exactly as it was designed to work. Your job is not to stop the shedding. Your job is to help the coat do its job. Brush regularly. Keep the undercoat loose. Watch for health signals. Accept the shedding. And trust that the double coat knows what it is doing.
